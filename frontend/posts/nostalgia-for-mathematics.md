It's been a while. Between getting married, working a full-time job, part-time grad school,
and part-time dance instructor, I have been struggling to create the space to write. While I can not
yet get back to a consistent post per week yet, I am hoping to create more space for writing in
public.

---

I just started a financial management course for my graduate program. One of the central themes in
financial management is calculating net present value (NPV), internal rate of return (IRR), and
similar. In past project management and portfolio management classes, I have used Excel and their
built in formulas to build out spreadsheets to answer these questions. But for the financial
management class, I'll need to use a calculator for exams.

Instead of buying a TI BA II as the course mentioned, I thought I would pull out my graphing
calculator from high school. At the time, I bought the cutting edge TI-nspire CX, which was more
calculator than I ended up using. However, it seemed like it may have the finance calculations that
I might need from a $40 finance-specific calculator, since I didn't need a calculator that was
allowed for specific exams like CPAs. 

As I pulled out my graphing calculator, the plastic sliding on plastic transported me back to
sitting in class, working through mathematics for calculus and physics. It was the strongest pull of
nostalgia that I have ever felt.

Life was simple. Each problem was bounded, containing a whole world. Nothing existed outside of the
pencil, paper, book, notes, and the calculator. Every time that I slide open the calculator, I have
deep feeling of longing--almost anguish--to re-live that experience; to be consumed with the
problem at hand and to . The flow state is where humans are happy, and at the end of every problem 

I wonder if this feeling is the introvert in me, longing for the days of less interpersonal
interactions (which come with a high correlation to emotional exhaustion, the first stage of
burnout). Or maybe it is the yearning for the flow state, which often doesn't exist for me in
management.

It is rare to feel such as strong of an emotional pull to something in the past as I have felt in
interacting with this calculator. This might be something to explore. How do I create space to do
more mathematics in my life? Or is this a combination of burnout, ambiguity, with a sprinkle of
nostalgia?
