It's been a while. Between getting married, working a full-time job, part-time grad school, and
part-time dance instructor, I have been struggling to create the space to write. While I cannot yet
get back to a consistent post per week, I am hoping to create more space for writing in public.

---

I just started a financial management course for my graduate program. One of the central themes in
financial management is calculating net present value (NPV), internal rate of return (IRR), and
similar. In past project management and portfolio management classes, I have used Excel and their
built-in formulas to build out spreadsheets to answer these questions. But for the financial
management class, I'll need to use a calculator for exams.

Instead of buying a TI BA II as the course mentioned, I thought I would pull out my graphing
calculator from high school. At the time, I bought the cutting-edge TI-nspire CX, which was more
calculator than I ended up using. Since it was such an overkill calculator for calculus and physics,
I thought that it might have the financial calculations that I need for this class. I am not
constrained by a certified calculator for a specific exam (such as a CPA), so if the Nspire supports
the financial calculations, then I could save $40 on the BA II.

As I pulled out my graphing calculator, the plastic sliding on plastic transported me back to
sitting in class, working through mathematics for calculus and physics. It was the strongest pull of
nostalgia that I have ever felt.

Life was simple. Each problem was bounded, containing a whole world. Nothing existed outside of the
pencil, paper, book, notes, and the calculator. Every time that I slide open the calculator, I have
a deep feeling of longing--almost anguish--to relive that experience; to be consumed with the
problem at hand. The flow state is where humans are happy, and at the end of every problem there is
a dopamine hit from seeing a hard problem all the way through to the solution.

I wonder if the feeling of nostalgia is the introvert in me, longing for the days of less
interpersonal interaction (which is highly correlated to emotional exhaustion, the first stage of
burnout). Or maybe it is the yearning for the flow state, which often doesn't exist for me in
management.

It is rare for me to feel such a strong emotional pull towards something in the past as I have felt
in interacting with this calculator. This might be something to explore. How do I create space to do
more mathematics in my life? Or is this a combination of burnout and ambiguity, with a sprinkle of
nostalgia?

Something similar hit towards the end of my previous job. I splurged on a handful of advanced books
on mathematics (complex analysis, abstract algebra, etc). However, when I sat down with my old
calculus book and worked through the intro/review chapter, I realized that I had forgotten most of
the intricacies over the years. I could not recall the quadratic formula without looking it up
(something that I learned in 6th grade), let alone L'Hôpital's rule. While the desire was to review
and build from where I had been in university, I found the friction was too high at that time.

However, thinking back, I wonder if I chose the wrong area to start with? I found calculus and most
other applied mathematics courses hard to keep motivated. But the theoretical classes that were
centered around proofs were where I shined. Instead of choosing a low-friction re-entry point, I
chose the highest.

I do not think there is any lifestyle change coming soon around more mathematics, but when this
nostalgia hits again--which I am sure it will--starting with something more theoretical may help get
through the initial startup friction to the point of building a habit.
