6:   Discussing my failure of cultivating team discussions and building trust by leaning too heavily
7:   into my comfort zone of one-on-one discussions
8: 
9: ---
10: 
11: The last failure that we will review is my failure to cultivate high performing teamwork between
12: team members. With my preparation to depart and as I was examining the communication structures on
13: my team. I observed a hub-and-spoke communication structure where a lot of the communication on the
14: team was passed through me. Hub-and-spoke communication structures bottleneck have an extremely high
15: chance of bottlenecking at the hub. 
16: 
17: High functioning teams operate smoothly more often than not. Each member knows their role and how
18: they fit into the team. They know the purpose of the team and how their contributions to the team
19: contribute to the overall organization's goals. These teams are characterized with high levels of
20: trust; each team member knowing that their teammates are executing their roles and that they have
21: their back (Sinek 2017).
22: 
23: Team meetings are one of the most important areas to build trust in fully remote and globally
24: distributed teams. Trust is an integral part of communication, and communication is an integral part
25: of trust. I have previously discussed the nuances of trust and communication--specifically effective
26: feedback--in [Management Trust Feedback Loop](/posts/management-trust-feedback-loop). 
27: 
28: Team meetings are a chance for team members to build trust. They provide a space to discuss the
29: different solutions to problems that the team needs to solve. It requires vulnerability to pitch an
30: idea for others to give feedback on and to build upon. Similarly to the relationship of
31: communication and trust, vulnerability increases trust and trust increases the ability to be
32: vulnerable. Cultivating the spaces for vulnerability and trust building in team meetings strengthens
33: the bonds of teamwork throughout the team.
34: 
35: Psychologically safe teams have been shown to be the highest performing teams; teams that deliver
36: the most business value in an efficient manner (Edmondson 2019). As such, my number one priority as
37: a manager is to create a psychologically safe space on the team to maximize the performance of my
38: team.
39: 
40: My focus over the last few years on cultivating psychological safety has been to make sure that
41: everyone feels heard. I think that I successfully achieved this goal, as discussed earlier on in
42: this series ([Success - Psychological Safety](/posts/retro-success-psychological-safety)).
43: However, I missed the important part of developing the skills in the rest of the team. My focus was
44: on my one-on-one relationships with each engineer on team to make sure that I not only heard and
45: understood them, but they felt heard and understood. I missed the opportunity to coach and develop
46: these same skills throughout the rest of the team.
47: 
48: A key piece of feedback that I received from my team was that a lot of discussions were happening
49: one-on-one and not happening as a team. As I reflected over this feedback, I realized that I had
50: been leaning into my communication strengths of one-on-one conversations rather than growing my
51: communication skills to effectively facilitate team discussions.
52: 
53: In the early days of moving from a peer engineer to being a manager, I prioritized the team feeling
54: heard above everything. My relationship on the team had just drastically change and essentially
55: started over and this was the most important thing that I felt that I needed to do to best position
56: the team to continue growing (I still feel that this was the best decision).
57: 
58: To cultivate the space for psychological safety, I regularly asked for questions, comments,
59: concerns, or ideas; sometimes multiple times a meeting. The majority of the time, this request was
60: met with silence, indicating to me and the rest of the team that there was nothing to add. I did not
61: fully believe that there was nothing to add, so I started re-asking these same questions in
62: 1:1s where it might be more comfortable for someone to share their thoughts. 
63: 
64: Over time, the quieter engineers learned that I would circle back to the important questions and
65: re-ask them in 1:1s, so would decide not to answer the questions in the team meetings and rather
66: save their input until the 1:1s. My comfort zone is the one-on-one conversations, so these would be
67: really valuable conversations, but would have very little transparency to the rest of the team.
68: 
69: This pattern is a pretty inefficient communication pattern where the hub is aware of everything
70: going on, but the spokes sometimes are not. This inefficient pattern is exacerbated with breadth of
71: focus of work on the team and any increase in pressure.
72: 
73: 
74: ## Gratitude
75: 
76: I am grateful that I have learned both the strength and weaknesses of being a one-on-one
77: communicator. Being aware of the importance of team discussions over one-on-one discussions is
78: helpful in identifying the skills that I need to grow in. 
79: 
80: In the future, I am curious if adopting some asynchronous communication processes would help in
81: synchronous communication. There is an idea of completing the communication loop by requiring a
82: response in asynchronous communication. Instant messaging applications are increasingly
83: becoming the backbone of organizational communication. In such an environment, if I send a message,
84: how do I know someone has received and read a message versus it sitting in their inbox? How does an
85: FYI message that does not include a call to action change this? Requiring a response, even if it is
86: just a specific emoji, communicates back that the message has been received and read. 
87: 
88: I wonder if this is an option in synchronous meetings, where we go around the table (in-person) or
89: through a static-order circular list (remote) to sound off with any questions, comments, concerns or
90: ideas. A "pass" acknowledges that the information has been received and there is nothing to add. In
91: addition to this, being careful at approaching one-on-one discussions on the same topics is
92: important. Any side discussions must be scrutinized to make sure they should be taken place outside
93: of the team discussion. If there is any information that surfaces that is important, it should be
94: brought back to the team by the source of the information (i.e. if an engineer has an idea, they
95: should be the one to share it with the rest of the group in a team meeting rather than the manager).
